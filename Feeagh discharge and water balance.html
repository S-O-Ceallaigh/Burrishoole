To check how well estimated Feeagh discharge (calling this Qo from now on) compared to anticipated outflow, I looked at the rainfall rate (using the Met Eireann rain gauge at the Furnace met station) over the 2 year period Jan '16 -> Dec '17 and also an estimate of evaporation

Mean Qo for the period 2016-2017 was 4.2 $m^{3}$ $s^{-1}$. 3295.8 mm of rain fell over the two year period (731 days). Taking 100 $km^{2}$ as the drainage basin area into Feeagh:

In summary, over this period the anticipated mean outflow based on the simplistic rainfall-evaporation model was 5.2 (rain) - 1.5 (evap) = 3.7. The mean estimated discharge through the Feeagh outflows was 4.2, so they differ by 0.5 $m^{3}$ $s^{-1}$ - is this a reasonable difference? (Slightly concerning as I think the water balance based on the rain-evap should be higher than the flow measurement estimates for discharge). However there are a couple of things to consider:

  1. The flow measurements will have an error range (e.g. may overestimate the higher river flows)
  2. There will surely be orographic enhancement of rainfall around the catchment - I only used a single rain gauge at ~ 10 m above sea level as being representative of rainfall over the whole catchment
  3. The evaporation method I used probably overestimated rates of evaporation (see above)

Also keep in mind these are only for steady state mean flow conditions over the 2 years - I have not assessed the difference for individual high/low flow events...

Assuming for now that the Feeagh outflow (Qo) is okay, possible to work backwards and figure out the inflows from the water balance equation under equilibrium conditions:¶

$Total In = dV/dt + Total Out$

where $Total In =$ stream inflows (Qi) $+$ direct precipitation on Feeagh ($P$), $dV/dt =$ change in the surface volume and $Total Out =$ stream outflows (Qo) $+$ lake surface evaporation ($E$).

An appropriate timescale for equilibrium is probably at least 17 hours based on lag times between rainfall events and water level peak increases I saw with the cross-correlation analysis; so for now am averaging everything to a daily timestep.

Here's the variables used for the water balance equation and how they were derived:

Looking at the above summary of Q_in for Feeagh in the output table above - do these seem like reasonable values for discharge from the Black and Glenamong? (bear in mind that the calculation method will encapsulate some of the discharge from e.g. the Cottage River/small streams that run into Feeagh, so the Qin reported here will probably be a little bit higher than that anticipated for just the Black and Glenamong)

There are a couple of things I am unsure about here that would be good to get some of your thoughts on:

  1. Negative value for Qin (spring 2016). This is obviously not possible and is related to an event where a large quantity of rain fell in a very short period (I think 13.2 mm in one hour at one point) and when the lake level (and stream inflows) had been dropping significantly prior to this. The sudden volume flux from direct rainfall over Feeagh surface exceeds the instantaneous inflow (this is possible as the flow/lake level volume change lags rain events by several hours but obviously inflow can't be negative at any point). This may be one of the limitations of this method (but doesn't appear to happen often).
  1. The Qin max flows exceed the Qout max flows which is curious - does this make sense? In one way, I think it does,if the storage capacity of the lake lets it act like a big buffer zone. When inflow >>> outflow, the lake level rises but there may be a bigger lag in the outflow of this water from the lake compared to the time taken for the level to rise due to inflow. The Mill Race and Salmon Leap are relatively narrow and constricted compared to the Black inflow, which may restrict the instantaneous rate of outflow by comparison. So if you get a huge discharge (vol of water per second) into Feeagh at the upper end then the buffering effect of the lake should reduce the outflow rate downstream by comparison (I think!). For steady-state conditions, the overall outflow will be equal (or greater), which it is averaged over time (4.2 vs 4.0 as outflow also includes direct Precip.). But perhaps the instantaneous flow rate can be greater for the unrestricted inflows, if Feeagh acts like a big low-pass filter for these incoming flows.

One way to potentially mitigate this is to use larger averaging intervals (2 or 3 days) or for daily resolution, bandpass filter the lake level timeseries with a cut-off frequency of ~3-5 days (see below). This is probably a good conservative place to start, as it represented the higher end of the time lag I saw between rain events and peak flows hence no negative values(it still shows slightly higher max inflows however so perhaps this is a reality of the system - the important thing is the mean outflows from Feeagh are slightly higher overall).

Okay let me know what ye think - does this make sense? I have used some simple assumptions here at times so perhaps you can help improve this and the catchment hydrology stuff is pretty new to me so I may have made some major mistake along the way!
